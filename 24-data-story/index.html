A scroll-driven cinematic data piece. Five centuries of North Atlantic winter temperatures, told in five chapters. The whole story lives in a single line — you draw it as you read.

§ Chapter 01 · 1525

The long winter, and its quiet beginning.

In 1525 the first written record of a "frozen sound" from a village in Northern Denmark. It reads, in translation: "On the eve of the feast, the boys walked across the water to the other shore."

The line begins here. Draw slowly.

Records · North Sea Weather Registry · 1525
§ Chapter 02 · 1709

The Great Frost.

Fifteen weeks below zero. Every port from Bergen to Bordeaux frozen. The Baltic walked, according to a Latvian shipping record: "one could take the harbour to the horizon."

Ninety percent of France's fruit trees were lost. Recovery began in 1712 and required a change to the tax code.

Records · Le Registre Général · 1709 winter
§ Chapter 03 · 1963

The last serious cold.

The winter of 1963 was cold enough that the Thames iced at Kingston. It is the last winter on the record to have done so.

The line, at this point, has begun to bend.

Records · UK Meteorological Office · 1963
§ Chapter 04 · 2003 → present

The bend, and what to do after.

By 2003 the North Atlantic winter no longer resembled its own precedent. The line is now bending upward at a rate of 0.24 degrees per decade — a slope that, five centuries earlier, we would not have known how to name.

Recovery, this time, will not be a matter of changing the tax code. It will be a matter of how we choose to remember the villages we already do not visit in winter.

Records · NOAA · Copernicus C3S · 2003–present
§ Chapter 05 · Five centuries

The whole line, at once.

You have drawn a line five hundred years long. This is what it looks like when you step back.

Every winter — from the boys walking the sound in 1525 to a scientist writing in Boulder in 2026 — is a single point on the same line.
